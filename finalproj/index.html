CS 184 Final Project Proposal -  Spring 2022

Bubblz

OVERVIEW

Title: Bubblz: Thin-film diffraction and particle-level motion

Description: Our goal is to simulate the movement and appearance of soap membrane bubbles. This project poses a unique challenge because soap bubbles have an iridescent and transparent surface, and also move with the wind. Our deliverable would be a scene where bubbles are blown and simulated over time as they move and burst.

Team members: Sravya Basvapatri, Tina Li, Simon Tsui, Daniel Won

PROBLEM DESCRIPTION

The challenge: Simulating how bubble blowing will require us to draw from various different topics covered in CS184. To make the bubbles look realistic, we will need to implement a BSDF that captures their iridescent and transparent surface, extending on implementations in Project 3-2. There’s also particle simulation, which builds upon ClothSim in Project 4 to simulate movement and potentially self-collisions for another type of material. Our project will have to account for the bubbles being created and popped on the screen. Additional factors will also play into the animation such as wind speed and wind direction, which will likely need to be represented in a simplified model to allow for real-time (or decently fast) rendering.

Importance of Project: Simulating and displaying real world phenomena is one of the main goals of animation. Particle motion is everywhere in animation, such as when simulating the movement of clothing or fluids. Bubble movement is just one example of visualizing objects in animation, and particularly interesting to us because it involves rendering iridescent surfaces, which we have not worked with so far in the class.

General idea on how to solve: There’s a lot of physics behind making the bubbles realistic.  Because this is a simulation of real life bubble blowing, this project will be dependent on learning and encoding the physics of bubble behavior. This likely includes encoding the Navier–Stokes equations, which are partial differential equations that describe the motion of viscous fluid substances. On the surface rendering side, it will also require understanding thin-membrane diffraction and reflection on a bubble’s surface.

GOALS AND DELIVERABLES

Final Project: Currently, our vision for the end product is a video simulation of the movement of bubbles. To incorporate interactivity as a stretch goal, we could have the user define the direction and strength of wind within the simulation, the speed at which bubbles are added to the simulation, or even the size of bubbles. We would likely start with bubbles against a solid color or black background to simplify  the task, and then try to move into rendering bubbles in other environments. Below are some still images as an example of what we hope to animate.

Measuring the quality / performance of your system: The quality of our project will be defined by a variety of different metrics:

  1. First, we need to ensure that our way of implementing thin-film diffraction or the way that the bubbles either reflect or transmit light is accurate to their real-world physical properties, demonstrating that certain wavelengths for colors are attenuated while others are reduced to show a prism-like surface rather than a pure transparent sphere, such as a glass ball. These colors should appear as fringes or waves along the surface of the bubble to reflect the different reflected light waves enhancing or canceling each other out.
  2. Another metric with respect to the bubbles’ particle simulation is that we can observe the bubbles’ trajectory and compare them with real-life demonstrations of how bubbles move when they are blown or travel through the air. For example, if the bubble is moving at a constant speed in a perfectly linear path (or a path that appears like a clear, distinct shape) rather than a visually semi-random trajectory, we can determine that our implementation of the physics simulation may be wrong. To also measure the performance of these simulations, we can keep track of the framerate (either via debugging tools or observation) at which the movement occurs to measure the efficiency of our code.

(1) The Baseline Goals (what you plan to deliver, questions we want to answer, and challenges we might to address):

  1. Simulation of iridescent surfaces. For thin-membrane surfaces like bubbles, this involves modeling thin-film diffraction and reflection, investigating how light passes through and reflects off of soap bubbles to create their appearance. Some problems we might encounter within this goal is simplifying wavelength dependence to the RGB representation we have within our current path-tracing model. We might need to explore additional color representations to achieve a realistic appearance. Another challenge of bubble simulation is the resolution required to properly represent bubbles. We need to subdivide the space into a 3D grid, which poses a challenge because bubbles are extremely thin, and would demand a high-resolution grid.
  2. Movement of bubbles through air. Our second goal is to add motion to our project, combining rendering with movement to tie together what we have learned in Projects 3 and 4. We would also like to examine the physical properties of soap bubbles that dictate the speed and trajectory at which it travels through the air for the particle simulation portion of our project. A challenge here we believe might be the speed at which the algorithm runs. If we want to have bubbles respond to the speed of the wind at different parts of 3D space, there might be a big storage and computational component to rendering movement. To avoid this, we need to look into simplified models to represent particle interactions.

(2) The Aspirational Plan (what you hope to deliver):

  1. Animation of a familiar scene. A scene many Berkeley students may be familiar with is the bubbles blowing from the apartment window through the streets at the intersection of Durant/Telegraph. We are hoping to implement particle-simulated animation for a scene with bubbles on the streets of Telegraph as a stretch goal, allowing our group members to further explore an interest in animation and art.
  2. Collision of bubbles.  The shape of soap bubbles over time seeks to reduce their surface area but retain volume over time. When bubbles collide, they do so while trying to maintain this property. We came across a Two Minute Paper video that explores the collision of bubbles and how they transform upon collision. The speed of rendering this phenomena is also an interesting challenge. We would love to recreate and build upon the methods described in this paper as time allows.
  3. Bubbles within different temperatures. Inspired by these videos of soap bubbles freezing in -15 degrees Celsius, a much further stretch goal would involve simulating bubbles within different temperatures, especially in colder temperatures when they freeze.
  4. Bubbles submerged in different fluids. According to Wikipedia (linked below), bubbles are visible because they have a different refractive index than the surrounding substance. Underwater, the RI of air is ~1.0003 and the RI of water is ~1.333. As we implemented in Project 4, Snell's Law describes how light changes direction between two mediums with different RI, composed of refraction and internal reflection. However, the soap bubbles we want to render does not distort light much, and is largely a result of diffraction and reflection. As another stretch goal, we could extend our research to try to render air bubbles submerged in other fluids, like water.
